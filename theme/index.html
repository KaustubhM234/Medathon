Tinnitus

Tinnitus is the perception of sound in the absence of an external source. The most common symptom of tinnitus is a ringing sound in the ears, but other sounds such as buzzing, hissing, whistling, or roaring can also be heard. Tinnitus can be intermittent or continuous, and can vary in loudness. It is often worse when background noise is low, so you may be most aware of it at night when you're trying to fall asleep in a quiet room.

Tinnitus is a common problem, affecting an estimated 15% of adults in the United States. In most cases, tinnitus is not a sign of a serious medical condition. However, it can be very bothersome and can interfere with sleep, concentration, and quality of life.

The prevalence of tinnitus can vary significantly depending on factors such as age, gender, and exposure to loud noises. In general, it becomes more common with age and is more prevalent among older adults.

Symptoms

The exact cause of tinnitus is unknown, but it is thought to be caused by damage to the hair cells in the inner ear.
This damage can be caused by a number of things, including:

  • Age-related hearing loss
  • Loud noise exposure
  • Ear infections
  • Medications
  • Tumors
  • Degenerative diseases such as Meniere's disease
  • Temporomandibular joint disorder (TMJ)
  • High blood pressure
  • Anxiety or stress

Treatment

There is no cure for tinnitus, but there are a number of treatments that can help to manage the symptoms.
These treatments include:

Sound therapy

This involves listening to calming sounds, such as white noise or nature sounds, to mask the tinnitus.

Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT):

This type of therapy can help you to manage your anxiety and stress, which can make tinnitus worse.

Medications

Some medications, such as anticonvulsants and antidepressants, can help to reduce the severity of tinnitus.

Tinnitus retraining therapy (TRT)

This involves using a combination of sound therapy and CBT to help you to adjust to the tinnitus and learn to ignore it.

Prevalence of Tinnitus

The prevalence of tinnitus can vary significantly depending on factors such as age, gender, and exposure to loud noises.

In general, it becomes more common with age and is more prevalent among older adults.
Here are some general statistics on the prevalence of tinnitus:

  • Overall Prevalence: Studies suggest that approximately 10-15% of the general population experience some form of tinnitus.
  • Age-Related Prevalence: Tinnitus becomes more common with increasing age. It is estimated that around 25-30% of people aged 65 and older experience tinnitus.
  • Gender Differences: Some research has suggested that men may be more likely to experience tinnitus than women.
  • Noise-Related Prevalence: Tinnitus is often associated with exposure to loud noises, such as from occupational or recreational activities. Individuals in professions with high noise exposure, such as construction or music, may have a higher risk of developing tinnitus.
  • Temporary vs. Chronic Tinnitus: Many people experience temporary tinnitus after exposure to loud noises, but for some, the condition becomes chronic and persists over time.

FAQ

Does tinnitus cause hearing loss

No. However, tinnitus is often a symptom of impending or developing hearing impairment. For that reason, if you begin to experience persistent, unexplained tinnitus, you may want to schedule an appointment with an audiologist.

How long does tinnitus last?

It varies. Tinnitus may occur only briefly, last for several months, or even be a lifetime ailment.
There are generally three classifications of tinnitus in this regard:
  • Acute tinnitus persists for less than three months and often stops spontaneously.
  • Subacute tinnitus reoccurs over a 3-12 month timeframe.
  • Chronic tinnitus persists for over twelve months.

Can I live normal life with tinnitus?

While there is no known cure for tinnitus, there are steps you can take to help alleviate the symptoms so you can live a normal life.
Eg: Have white noise playing in the background to help drown out the sounds in your head.
